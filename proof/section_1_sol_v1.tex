**Proof.** We use **Euclid’s lemma**: if a prime number $r$ divides a product $ab$, then $r$ divides $a$ or $r$ divides $b$. ([proofwiki.org](https://proofwiki.org/wiki/Euclid%27s_Lemma_for_Prime_Divisors?utm_source=openai))

Assume, for contradiction, that $\sqrt{7}$ is rational. Then there exist integers $p$ and $q$ with $q \neq 0$ such that

\[
\sqrt{7}=\frac{p}{q},
\]

and we may choose $p$ and $q$ so that

\[
\gcd(p,q)=1.
\]

Squaring both sides gives

\[
7=\frac{p^2}{q^2}.
\]

Multiplying by $q^2$ gives

\[
p^2=7q^2.
\]

Thus

\[
7 \mid p^2.
\]

Since $p^2=p\cdot p$ and $7$ is prime, Euclid’s lemma implies that

\[
7 \mid p.
\]

Therefore, for some integer $k$,

\[
p=7k.
\]

Substituting this into $p^2=7q^2$ gives

\[
(7k)^2=7q^2.
\]

Hence

\[
49k^2=7q^2.
\]

Dividing by $7$ gives

\[
7k^2=q^2.
\]

So

\[
7 \mid q^2.
\]

Again, since $7$ is prime, Euclid’s lemma implies that

\[
7 \mid q.
\]

Thus $7$ divides both $p$ and $q$, contradicting the assumption that

\[
\gcd(p,q)=1.
\]

Therefore, our assumption that $\sqrt{7}$ is rational is false. Hence

\[
\boxed{\sqrt{7}\text{ is irrational}.}
\]
