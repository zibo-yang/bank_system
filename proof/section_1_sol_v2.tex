The reported gap is valid: the reduced-form representation should be justified. Here is a self-contained proof.

**Proof.** We first justify the “lowest terms” step.

**Lemma 1.** If \(x\in \mathbb{Q}\), then \(x=\frac{p}{q}\) for some integers \(p,q\) with \(q>0\), such that no integer \(d>1\) divides both \(p\) and \(q\).

**Proof of Lemma 1.** Since \(x\in \mathbb{Q}\), there exist integers \(a,b\) with \(b\neq 0\) such that

\[
x=\frac{a}{b}.
\]

If \(b<0\), replace \(a,b\) by \(-a,-b\), so we may assume \(b>0\). Consider the nonempty set

\[
S=\left\{n\in \mathbb{Z}_{>0} : x=\frac{m}{n}\text{ for some }m\in \mathbb{Z}\right\}.
\]

This set is nonempty because \(b\in S\). By the well-ordering principle, \(S\) has a least element; call it \(q\). Choose \(p\in \mathbb{Z}\) such that

\[
x=\frac{p}{q}.
\]

Suppose some integer \(d>1\) divides both \(p\) and \(q\). Then \(p=dp'\) and \(q=dq'\) for some integers \(p',q'\). Since \(q>0\) and \(d>1\), we have \(q'>0\) and \(q'<q\). Also,

\[
x=\frac{p}{q}=\frac{dp'}{dq'}=\frac{p'}{q'}.
\]

Thus \(q'\in S\), contradicting the minimality of \(q\). Therefore no integer \(d>1\) divides both \(p\) and \(q\). \(\square\)

We also need the following elementary divisibility fact.

**Lemma 2.** If \(7\mid n^2\), then \(7\mid n\).

**Proof of Lemma 2.** By the division algorithm, every integer \(n\) can be written as

\[
n=7t+r
\]

for some integer \(t\) and some \(r\in \{0,1,2,3,4,5,6\}\). Then

\[
n^2 \equiv r^2 \pmod 7.
\]

The possible squares modulo \(7\) are

\[
0^2\equiv 0,\quad
1^2\equiv 1,\quad
2^2\equiv 4,\quad
3^2\equiv 2,\quad
4^2\equiv 2,\quad
5^2\equiv 4,\quad
6^2\equiv 1
\pmod 7.
\]

Thus the only way \(n^2\equiv 0\pmod 7\) is if \(r=0\). Hence \(7\mid n\). \(\square\)

Now suppose, for contradiction, that \(\sqrt{7}\) is rational. By Lemma 1, we may write

\[
\sqrt{7}=\frac{p}{q}
\]

where \(p,q\in \mathbb{Z}\), \(q>0\), and no integer \(d>1\) divides both \(p\) and \(q\).

Squaring both sides gives

\[
7=\frac{p^2}{q^2}.
\]

Since \(q\neq 0\), multiplying by \(q^2\) gives

\[
p^2=7q^2.
\]

Therefore,

\[
7\mid p^2.
\]

By Lemma 2, this implies

\[
7\mid p.
\]

So there exists an integer \(k\) such that

\[
p=7k.
\]

Substituting this into \(p^2=7q^2\), we get

\[
(7k)^2=7q^2.
\]

Thus

\[
49k^2=7q^2.
\]

Dividing by \(7\), we obtain

\[
7k^2=q^2.
\]

Hence

\[
7\mid q^2.
\]

By Lemma 2 again,

\[
7\mid q.
\]

So \(7\) divides both \(p\) and \(q\). This contradicts our choice of \(p\) and \(q\) as having no common divisor greater than \(1\).

Therefore the assumption that \(\sqrt{7}\) is rational is false. Hence

\[
\boxed{\sqrt{7}\text{ is irrational}.}
\]
